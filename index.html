    Why a GPX, not Maps

    Built for driving, not commuting

    No tapping mid-corner

    Google Maps pauses at every waypoint and waits for you to hit Continue. A GPX is one unbroken line — no looking down, no reaching for the screen between stops.

    See the corner before it arrives

    Turn-by-turn gives you one arrow at a time. A track shows the true shape of the road ahead, so you can read a corner early and carry speed through it.

    It won’t reroute you

    No “faster route available,” no quiet detours onto the highway. The GPX holds the exact backroad you chose — every stop and turn, in the order you set.

    Works with no signal

    The best roads run through canyons and ranch land with no bars. The track lives on your device and keeps guiding you where live navigation quits.

    Pace notes, not POIs

    Drop the track into an app like Sideways and it reads the route as rally pace notes — corner severity and distance to the next — instead of ads and fuel-stop pins.

    Save it, share it, run it again

    A GPX is just a file. Re-run your favorite loop, swap stages with friends, or build a whole library of roads worth driving.

    Everything runs in your browser — nothing you paste is kept. Routes are looked up with free public services, so the occasional link may need a second try.
